Demographic differences in health and health finances among Medicare beneficiaries


Insights from the Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey (2021)

Background

What is Medicare?


  • Medicare is a federal public health insurance program in the U.S. that serves adults who are older than 65 or who are younger than 65 and have a disability.
  • Medicare is the largest health insurer in the U.S., paying billions to hospitals and other health care providers each year.
  • These activities are funded by payroll taxes.
  • Over 67.7 million individuals are enrolled in Medicare, including 89.4% of adults 65 and older.
  • We can learn more about these beneficiaries with the Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey.

Demographics

What did we notice?


  • The Deep South and Appalachia have higher concentrations of beneficiaries with disabilities and correspondingly beneficiaries less than 65, with Arkansas leading at 19.2%. These areas also report elevated rates of chronic kidney disease, highlighting regional health challenges.
  • There is a higher proportion of Black beneficiaries in the South, including states like Mississippi, Louisiana, and Georgia. The highest concentration reaches 32% in Mississippi, which happens to be the state with the highest Black population.
  • In a similar reflection of national census data, most Hispanic-identifying beneficiaries live the southwest. The proportion of Hispanic respondents tops out at 32% in New Mexico; similar proportions are seen in neighboring states.
  • The survey gender ratio is roughly 85 men for 100 women, slightly outpacing the national gender ratio (~50%) and reflecting womenâ€™s longer life expectancy.

Health and satisfaction

How do health and satisfaction vary by race?


  • The donut charts below show beneficiary chronic conditions (inner circles) and satisfaction (outer circle) across races. Depending on your screen size, you may need to scroll sideways to see all of the donuts.
  • The proportion of beneficiaries with that chronic condition or satisfaction level maps onto how far that ring goes around the circle. For example, if the ring goes halfway around the circle, then it applies to half of beneficiaries.
  • Hovering over a ring shows you more information about it.

What did we notice?


  • The data identifies notable disparities in chronic health conditions and Medicare satisfaction across racial groups.
  • Black respondents report the highest rates of high blood pressure (75%) and Hispanic respondents report the highest rates of diabetes (44%).
  • Meanwhile, White respondents are the most likely to be very satisfied (54%) with Medicare and have the lowest rates of chronic illness.
  • Overall, Black and Hispanic people experience higher levels of chronic illness while having lower satisfaction with Medicare.

Finances

What is supplemental insurance?


  • Because Medicare does not cover all healthcare costs, most beneficiaries have supplemental insurance: one or more of Medicaid, Medicare Advantage, Part D, and private insurance.
  • Though eligibility varies by state, beneficiaries are also eligible for Medicaid if they make lower incomes. These "dual-eligibles" often face many compounding social risk factors that impact their health; for this reason, many public health interventions focus on this group.
  • For additional benefits like vision insurance, dental insurance, and/or care coordination, beneficiaries can opt for a privately-administered Medicare Advantage plan. (Trump and others in his party want to expand Medicare Advantage, though these plans are costlier and often provide fewer services.)
  • Due to unmanageably high prescription drug costs, 80.4% of beneficiaries also enroll in Part D insurance for assistance with this.
  • Finally, beneficiaries who still receive insurance from an employer are additionally privately insured.

How does supplemental coverage vary with demographics?


  • In this interactive visualization, you can explore supplemental coverage trends across demographics.
  • You can click on one of the demographic categories (first row) and many of the supplemental insurance types (second row) to compare the percent and number of beneficiaries with each type of coverage.
  • Additionally, you can click on the bars to learn what percent of those beneficiaries have trouble paying their medical bills. (For reference, 5.6% of beneficiaries without any supplemental insurance across demographic groups have trouble paying their medical bills.)

What did we notice?


  • Beneficiaries who are disabled, younger than 65, Black, Hispanic, and/or have a history of mental illness are more likely to be dual-eligible than other beneficiaries. These groups are in turn more likely to have trouble paying medical bills, likely as a result of Medicaid's strict asset limits.
  • Conversely, beneficiaries who are White and/or do not have a history of mental illness are least likely to be dual-eligible and most likely to be privately insured; correspondingly, these beneficiaries are less likely to have trouble paying medical bills.

How do out-of-pockets costs compare across demographics?

  • You can filter this graph by gender and/or race with the dropdown menus below.

Distribution of Out-of-Pocket Costs


What did we notice?


  • The majority of beneficiaries experience relatively low out-of-pocket expenses, with the highest frequency concentrated in the lowest cost category. As costs increase, the number of beneficiaries sharply declines, revealing a small but significant group incurring expenses above $10,000 in 2021. This emphasizes the uneven distribution of financial responsibility among Medicare beneficiaries.
  • White beneficiaries generally have higher out-of-pocket costs than non-white beneficiaries, with over 30 paying more than $35,000 in 2021.

What did we notice?


  • While higher total payments often correlate with increased out-of-pocket expenses, the data also uncovers income-based disparities:
    • Lower-income beneficiaries are concentrated in the lower payment ranges yet still face considerable variability in out-of-pocket costs.
    • Higher-income beneficiaries are more evenly distributed across the payment spectrum, with outliers incurring exceptionally high total payments and out-of-pocket costs.
  • These patterns underscore the challenges faced by lower-income individuals, who may be disproportionately burdened despite their lower total payments.

Summary

What are our main findings?


  • Medicare beneficiaries are geographically, demographically, and financially diverse.
  • People of color are more likely to face chronic health conditions and least likely to receive satisfactory services.
  • There are also demographic differences in supplemental insurance and out-of-pocket costs that systematically disenfranchise minority beneficiaries.

What is our key takeaway?


The geographic, demographic, and financial diversity of Medicare beneficiaries is critical to recognize and prioritize when making policy decisions. Only by understanding and addressing these differences can we achieve equitable access to high-quality health care and improve Medicare for all.
