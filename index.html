Demographic differences in health finances among Medicare beneficiaries


Insights from the Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey (2021)

What is Medicare?

  • Medicare is a federal public health insurance program in the U.S. that serves adults who are older than 65 or who have a disability.

  • Medicare is the largest health insurer in the U.S., paying billions to hospitals and other health care providers each year.

  • These activities are funded by payroll taxes.

  • Over 67.7 million individuals are enrolled in Medicare, including 89.4% of adults 65 and older.

  • We can learn more about these beneficiaries with the Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey.

What are the demographics of Medicare beneficiaries?

Who is eligible for Medicare?

Medicare is primarily available for people who are aged 65 or older. Provided that they are eligible for Social Security benefits, people with disabilities, permanent kidney failure, or ALS may be able to enroll for Medicare before they turn 65. We invite you to explore our interactive map to observe the geographic trends of Medicare eligibility. In America's Deep South and Appalachia regions, there is a higher concentration of people with disabilities who are Medicare beneficiaries, with Arkansas having the highest proportion at 19.2%. These areas also experience the highest amount of Chronic Kidney Disease according to the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Similar to the concentration of Medicare beneficiaries who have disabilities in the Deep South and Appalachia regions, most people younger than 65 years old who receive Medicare live here as well.

What is supplemental insurance?

  • Because Medicare does not cover all healthcare costs, most beneficiaries have supplemental insurance: one or more of Medicaid, Medicare Advantage, Part D, and private insurance.

    • Though eligibility varies by state, beneficiaries are also eligible for Medicaid if they make lower incomes.
      • These "dual-eligible" beneficiaries often face many compounding social risk factors that impact their health. For this reason, many public health interventions focus on improving health care for dual-eligibles.

    • For additional benefits like vision and dental insurance, beneficiaries can opt for a privately-administered Medicare Advantage plan.
      • Trump and supporters want to expand Medicare Advantage, though these plans are costlier and often provide fewer services.

    • Due to unmanageably high prescription drug costs, 80.4% of beneficiaries also enroll in Part D insurance for assistance paying for pharmaceuticals.

    • Finally, beneficiaries who still receive insurance from an employer are also privately insured.

How do out-of-pockets costs compare across demographics?

Building on the demographics of Medicare beneficiaries and their supplemental insurance coverage, the bar chart delves into the financial burden faced by beneficiaries in terms of out-of-pocket healthcare costs. The majority of beneficiaries experience relatively low out-of-pocket expenses, with the highest frequency concentrated in the lowest cost category. However, as costs increase, the number of beneficiaries sharply declines, revealing a small but significant group incurring expenses above $10,000. This highlights the uneven distribution of financial responsibility among Medicare beneficiaries, where a vulnerable minority bears disproportionately higher costs.
